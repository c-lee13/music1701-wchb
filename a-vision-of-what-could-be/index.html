A Vision of What Could Be

Breaking the Cycle of Violence

The Cycle

From the beginning of time, the two sister cities in Arcane – Piltover and Zaun – have been trapped in a cycle of hatred and destruction. Passed down countless generations, their respective inhabitants have long since forgotten the origins of this conflict. So thoroughly ingrained within their identities and lifestyles, most merely embrace this hate without ever questioning the reason behind it.

“What Could Have Been” echoes the inability of those born into this cycle to break free. The song bemoans how despite their awareness of the hurt they have caused and despite their longing for a better life, violence has become too intrinsic and they are powerless to change. 

However, in Arcane’s Season 2 Episode 7, we see an alternate reality that is a vibrant paradise, bustling with prosperity and joy. This new reality contrasts the originally bleak Piltover and Zaun, begetting the question of how it came to be.

Consequently, what does it take, and more importantly, what does it mean to break free of such a cycle of violence? 

 

Aspirations for Unity: Realized or Ruined?

The paradoxical imagery of the Bridge is the most striking difference between the two universes: in one, it is the site of prominent scenes of violence – a monument of inescapable divide. In the other, it is a lively marketplace where Piltovans and Zaunites trade with wide smiles and open hearts – a true landmark of prosperity.

But no matter its ideological significance, the Bridge’s physical structure is a steadfast constant. As all bridges are, it was built from the “desire for connection and movement” (Encinas Cantalapiedra 80) – a literal bridging of the geographical rift between the cities. It has always harbored the potential for unification, but whether this potential is fulfilled hinges on how the cities confront violence. 

 

Sacrificial Lamb

In the original universe, this potential is buried beneath chaos and calamity.

Blinded by their thirst for power over one another, the two cities have soon lost sight of their initial promise of harmony. Instead, a theme of bloodlust arises, as seen through Arcane’s ever escalating scenes of conflict and destruction. With each city possessing a sense of moral superiority over the other, a cycle of blame is borne – one in which retribution is pursued through the relentless spilling of blood.

This follows Encinas Cantalapiedra’s argument of how the world of Arcane is “governed by… the sacrifice of the guilty” and that this “execution [performs the] social function” (Encinas Cantalapiedra 80-81) of temporarily satiating this bloodlust. 

However, a crucial distinction must be made upon his argument: the person punished as “guilty” is often not guilty. 

In the alternate universe, Vi’s death is an instance of this “execution”. Her death – that of a child – serves the social purpose of exposing how innocents are caught in the crossfire of violence. 

As cruel as it may be, it is the necessary sacrifice that forces society to confront the gravity of its own injustice and inhumanity. By striking society’s conscience so profoundly that it sparks a movement toward reconciliation, Vi’s death ensures that no other innocent suffers the same fate in this needless war.

 

Forgiven, But Never Forgotten

The potential for progress is thus realized in this second universe, primarily demonstrated through Vander and Silco’s reconciliation: although injuries – Silco’s marred eye and Vander’s maimed arm – from their vicious brawl linger, they have found peace.

Mell-Taylor argues that Arcane presents an oversimplification of peace, where the show’s failure to “undo the generations of economic and political inequality” makes it unlikely to achieve the “permanent realignment” needed for an “end [to] the cycle of violence” (Alex Has Opinions 2025). 

Just as how the Bridge remains, a constant reminder of the bitter destruction of the past and of the unchangeable geographical separation of the cities, Vander and Silco’s previous mistakes are forever etched onto their souls. Mell-Taylor rightfully points out how it is impossible for such deeply ingrained wounds to ever be erased. 

However, though divide will always persist, that does not mean unity cannot be achieved. Unity is far from a complete “realignment,” which implies the elimination of all differences. Rather unity is the embrace of divide: the realization that despite this divide, there still is great potential for cohesion.

Therefore, paralleling how the Bridge has turned into a prosperous marketplace, the brothers’ acceptance of their grievous injuries as an irrevocable part of their identity, coupled with their strength to forgive, allows them not to let pain and anger corrupt their actions. Instead, by viewing these scars as symbols to inspire growth and positive change, they are finally able to break free from the cycle of violence.

 

Hope To Conquer Violence

It is unfair, and naive, to compare the world of Arcane to our modern reality. Arcane does not provide an explicit explanation for the resolution of material inequalities between Piltover and Zaun, because ultimately, it is a fictional story. 

But there is much to learn even from a work of fiction. 

In a world already rife with pessimism, is it not more beneficial to embrace the hope that Arcane offers, rather than succumb to a despair that begets a cycle of inaction and to think that we will inevitably “return to the status quo the day after disaster ends” (Alex Has Opinions 2025)? 

Arcane shows us what it means to be human: to be capable of great evil, but also great good. We cannot escape from our mistakes or loss, but we do have the power to choose how these experiences shape us. To have the strength to look past our grievances, to acknowledge our differences, and to seek unity despite them. To make the conscious decision to step out of the cycle of violence, and to hold onto hope that, through our efforts, a better future awaits.

This is the true essence of progress.

It may start small, like mending bonds between families. But this seemingly trivial step has the potential to ignite something far greater: true societal change.
