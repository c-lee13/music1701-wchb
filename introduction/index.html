Introduction

Echoes of "What Could Have Been"

In 2009, Riot Games released their first hit video game League of Legends. As a MOBA (multiplayer online battle arena) with each controllable “champion” possessing a unique skillset, League soon skyrocketed in popularity.

Naturally desiring to reach a broader audience, Riot Games began experimenting with adaptations of media outside of the game. The music video for “Warriors” by Imagine Dragons then became Riot’s first breakthrough, amassing over 440 million YouTube views since its release. Its widespread appeal stems from the experiential impact of music and animation on audiences. As Díaz Gasca (2013) defines, experientiality elicits two key emotional responses from audiences. The first is “character association,” which links specific sounds to characters and thereby reinforces audiences’ understanding of the characters’ emotions. The second is “nostalgia,” which evokes sentiments of longing or regret, hence enhancing audiences’ emotional connection to the narrative. By integrating League lore into music and animation, “Warriors” revealed a critical demand for experiential immersion into the game, thus playing a pivotal role in inspiring Riot’s first full-fledged animated television series: Arcane.

Arcane is a compelling narrative about how societal conflict (between two cities: the privileged Piltover and the oppressed Zaun) fractures the relationship between two sisters, Jinx and Vi. Their lives are thus shaped by war, betrayal, and the pursuit of vengeance. A recurring theme throughout Arcane’s narrative is consequently the cyclic nature of violence, whereby society’s infliction of harm upon families and individuals spurs them to pursue retribution through further violence. Music, predominantly Arcane’s soundtrack, plays an integral role in emphasizing this motif. Through the soundtrack’s experiential impact upon audiences, Arcane emphasizes its characters’ hatred and agony, highlighting how these corrosive emotions sweep them into the cycle of violence. 

Febrer-Coll (2023) provides a case study of how one of these songs, “Enemy” by Imagine Dragons, reflects how Jinx’s loss has transformed her into a volatile, hatred-driven individual – the perfect weapon to retaliate against Piltover’s brutality. In “When Story Meets Sound,” however, I propose examining how another song – “What Could Have Been” – further aids audiences in understanding the emotional motivations driving Jinx’s violent actions. 

Serving as the closing song of Season One, “What Could Have Been” by Sting (featuring violinist Ray Chen) reminds audiences of the cold hard reality that our actions have consequences – upon individuals, upon families, and upon societies. It does this by not only illuminating Jinx’s mental turmoil, but also transcending Arcane’s fictional narrative, evoking the audience’s personal regrets.

To depict violence’s impact on individuals, “What Could Have Been” employs Díaz Gasca’s “character association” through Jinx’s violin leitmotif – a “melodic phrase… (accompanying) the reappearance of … [a] person” (Merriam-Webster 2025). In “How Arcane Charts Tragedy,” I demonstrate how this association allows audiences to track Jinx’s mental state, with this moment marking her complete mental break. Bell Jordan’s (2016) analysis of the gender stereotypes around violin performance provides deeper insight into Arcane’s choice to represent Jinx with the violin. She reveals the traditional notion that the violin should be played solely by male violinists, for fear that its physical demand would render a woman uncouth. Women who overcome this restriction are inevitably shunned, signifying the perpetuation of discrimination. Similarly, Jinx is a victim of societal prejudice and is forced to wield violence to break free. Society then deems Jinx as monstrous, thus paralleling the perceived depravity of female violinists. This reinforces Arcane’s motif of violence, depicting how society’s violence against individuals breeds violence in individuals against society.

Furthermore, “What Could Have Been” also portrays Jinx's grievance against Vi. “The Cycle Only Ends When You Walk Away” reveals how the song’s spiteful lyrics reflect the societal strife that shaped the sisters’ conflicting ideologies, ultimately fueling their mutual resentment. Imbued with intense, mournful emotions, the song thus compels audiences to bemoan the destructive impact of violence upon families.

“How Arcane Charts Tragedy” then addresses the thought process of Alexander Seaver, Arcane’s executive music producer, in the making of “What Could Have Been.” He highlights how he deliberately manipulates the song’s instrumentals to enhance the scene’s tragedy, such as incorporating an intense violin solo. These changes effectively cause Jinx’s despair to resonate with audiences, thereby underscoring the soundtrack’s experiential impact. Furthermore, I contend that “What Could Have Been” demonstrates how multiple characters – not just Jinx – channel their grievances into violence, highlighting a shared, inescapable cycle of conflict that traps the entirety of humanity.

Even so, we must inquire if hope remains to break free of these chains of violence. “A Vision of What Can Be” subsequently explores the path towards peace as depicted in the final arc of Arcane’s Season Two. I first underscore the harsh reality that society will forever bear the scars of violence. Yet by examining the significance of forgiveness amidst suffering, I also demonstrate that the strength to forgive – rather than forget – the pain and mistakes of our past, will ultimately aid us in forging a better future. 

At the heart of Arcane’s Season One finale, the song “What Could Have Been” exemplifies music’s ability to convey characters’ emotions and evoke audiences’ empathy. This experiential influence upon audiences underscores the devastating repercussions of societal violence on individuals and families, alluding to how such injustice often forces those affected to perpetuate further violence. Focusing on this theme, my project delves into the emotional motivations and societal dynamics that sustain this cycle, whilst further exploring what it means to attain true healing within a divided society.
